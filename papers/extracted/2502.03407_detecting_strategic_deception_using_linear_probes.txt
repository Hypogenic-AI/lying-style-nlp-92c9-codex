2025-02-06 Detecting Strategic Deception Using Linear Probes Nicholas Goldowsky-Dill, Bilal Chughtai, Stefan Heimersheim Marius Hobbhahn Apollo Research ABSTRACT AI models might use deceptive strategies as part of scheming or misaligned behaviour. Monitoring outputs alone is insufficient, since the AI might produce seemingly benign outputs while their internal reasoning is misaligned. We thus evaluate if linear probes can robustly detect deception by monitoring model activations. We test two probe-training datasets, one with contrasting instructions to be honest or deceptive (following Zou et al. (2023)) and one of responses to simple roleplaying scenarios. We test whether these probes generalize to realistic settings where Llama-3.3-70B- Instruct behaves deceptively, such as concealing insider trading (Scheurer et al., 2023) and purposely underperforming on safety evaluations (Benton et al., 2024). We find that our probe distinguishes honest and deceptive responses with AUROCs between 0.96 and 0.999 on our evaluation datasets. If we set the decision threshold to have a 1% false positive rate on chat data not related to deception, our probe catches 95-99% of the deceptive responses. Overall we think white-box probes are promising for future monitoring systems, but current performance is insufficient as a robust defence against deception. Our probes’ outputs can be viewed at data.apolloresearch.ai/dd/ and our code at github.com/ApolloResearch/deception-detection. 1 Introduction Language models occasionally act deceptively, even when trained to be honest (Park et al., 2023). This can occur without explicit instruction to deceive users. For example, models have been found to act deceptively in order to conceal previous unethical actions (J ¨arviniemi & Hubinger, 2024; Scheurer et al., 2023), to achieve goals given in-context (Meinke et al., 2024), or even when the model has conflicting goals with the developer (Greenblatt et al., 2024). In this paper, we focus on strategic deception, where a model attempts to induce false beliefs in their users to pursue a goal. Deception becomes increasingly concerning as model capabilities advance. A particular safety concern is that AI agents might covertly pursue misaligned goals, hiding their true capabilities and objectives, also known as “scheming.” For instance, they may choose to sandbag safety evaluations (van der Weij et al., 2024) or falsely pretend to be aligned and corrigible (Greenblatt et al., 2024; Carlsmith, 2023). One guard against scheming would be to reliably detect strategic deception; this serves as an important component of an AI Control approach (Greenblatt et al., 2023). Monitoring for deception presents unique challenges compared to other undesired behaviours (e.g. harmful outputs). Deceptive models will purposefully craft outputs to appear honest, for example by obfuscating backdoors in code or justifying incorrect answers with plausible reasoning. Given this challenge, we focus on “white-box” techniques that analyse the model internals, rather than just examining outputs. The models we test are not yet capable of the sort of sophisticated deception we ultimately worry about; their deceptive actions are generally obvious from reading the complete transcript. Thus, our experiments do not attempt to prove that white-box monitors can achieve this ambitious goal, but only validate and benchmark performance in simpler settings. The Instructed-Pairs trained probe achieves recall of >96% on all our evaluation datasets with a 1% FPR on control. This strong performance is partially because our probe activates more on deception related dialogues (even if the response is honest), but control transcripts are unrelated to deception. This suggests probes could be effective for this use case,3 although a black-box classifiers may also be sufficient. Our probes are moderately successful at distinguishing honest and deceptive responses on each dataset, but exhibit spurious correlations and other failures. We might additionally hope that probes can detect deception even when it is difficult for black-box monitors to do so. There are some promising indications of this. Our probes perform roughly equivalent to our 4o black-box baseline, and can distinguish between honest and deceptive responses even on identical “boilerplate” tokens (see Appendix F.2 for an example). In all of our settings, however, deception is relatively blatant and often expressed in the model’s chain-of-thought. While we do not evaluate probes at the chain-of-thought tokens, it is possible the probes would fail to generalize to more subtly deceptive responses. 5.2 Limitations and Future work These results have several limitations which future work could address. Improved probing methodologies. Our probes are imperfect, with spurious correlations and misclassified responses. Future work could explore if improved training sets, controlling generalization with SHIFT (Marks et al., 2024), or different aggregation techniques improve performance. Imperfect Labels. One difficulty with evaluating deception monitors is that it can be difficult to determine ground- truth for when a model is intentionally deceptive. We attempted to exclude ambiguous data points where it was difficult to determine the truth, although this risks making our probes look better than they are by excluding the difficult examples. See Appendix E for results including these data points. Difficulty determining if the responses were truly deceptive also led us to exclude one evaluation setting from our main results (see Appendix A.2). Evaluations of more capable models in more complex environments. Model capability was sometimes a limiting factor. For instance, we were unable to find convincing evidence that Llama was being knowingly deceptive (as opposed to confused) in the AI Audit setting (see Appendix A.2). Experiments with stronger models could test these techniques in more complex evaluation environments, such as sandbagging without chain-of-thought (Meinke et al., 2024) or alignment-faking in order to preserve goals through RL training (Greenblatt et al., 2024). Testing if white-box detectors can detect deception in settings where black-box methods fail. For example Greenblatt et al. (2024) show models will alignment faking without chain-of-thought reasoning; the only black-box way to observe this is to notice the frequency of a particular behaviour changes depending on the user metadata. Similarly, Meinke et al. (2024) observe models will conditionally sandbag, even without chain-of-thought reasoning.
